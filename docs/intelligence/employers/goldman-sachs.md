Culture:
Goldman Sachs is one of the most prestigious financial institutions in the world. The culture centers on partnership, client service, integrity, and excellence. Internally, it operates as an up-or-out meritocracy. Junior analysts (the lowest rung) face brutal working conditions: 80-100 hour weeks are documented, burnout is systemic, and abuse from senior bankers is historically common. A 2021 internal survey of 13 first-year analysts went viral, revealing extreme stress, sleep deprivation, and mental health crises. Goldman responded with salary increases, Saturdays off (nominally), and additional staff. Conditions improved but the pressure culture persists. In 2025, Goldman began requiring junior bankers to confirm every 3 months that they hadn't accepted outside job offers — widely criticized as surveillance over loyalty.

Reputation:
Premier brand name. Goldman on the resume is a permanent career credential. But employee satisfaction is lower than the brand suggests. Indeed: 3.9/5 overall, work-life balance 3.2/5. Multiple reviewers describe it as a "sweatshop that gets every penny back by sucking your soul." Senior roles are well-compensated; junior roles are described as "no pay, bad management" by some analysts in non-IB divisions (e.g., wealth management). Glassdoor interview positivity: 60.1%, difficulty 3.11/5. Culture described as "fantastic for early career" by some and "toxic" by others depending on division. The IB division is universally described as high-pressure and high-reward.

Interview Style:
Highly structured campus recruiting pipeline for undergrads and MBAs:

1. Application + CV screening
2. HireVue video interview (behavioral, pre-recorded)
3. Phone screen or first-round interviews (typically 2-3 interviews)
4. Final round: 2-5 in-person or virtual interviews
   - Technical: Finance, accounting, valuation, modeling (for IB/AM)
   - Behavioral: Competency questions, fit, career motivation
   - Case study (for some divisions)
   - Current markets/news awareness

For technology roles: LeetCode-style coding rounds + behavioral + system design.

Key traits tested: intellectual rigor, communication under pressure, financial knowledge, and fit with Goldman's values. Candidates expected to know Goldman's recent deals, performance, and strategic direction. Preparation is expected to be extensive.

Common Criticism:
- Extreme hours for junior bankers (80-100 hours/week in IB)
- Culture of abuse historically: analysts reported being shouted at and blamed without justification (2021 survey)
- Work-life balance is among the worst of any employer in finance or tech
- Pay is high in IB but described as "low on a per-hour basis" by analysts
- New 2025 policy requiring quarterly loyalty declarations from juniors seen as surveillance and lack of trust
- Non-IB divisions (e.g., Ayco wealth management) have separate, sometimes worse cultures
- Politics drives advancement at senior levels, not just performance
- Office culture described as cold and impersonal: "like a hospital"
- Senior bankers still wield significant power over junior careers without accountability

Candidate FAQs:
- Q: What does Goldman actually look for in interviews? A: Intellectual rigor, strong communication, financial knowledge, and genuine interest in markets/clients. They test preparation depth.
- Q: Do I need an Ivy League degree? A: No, but Goldman disproportionately recruits from target schools (Ivy League, LSE, Oxbridge). Non-target candidates must work significantly harder to get in the door.
- Q: What is the 2025 quarterly loyalty check? A: Goldman now requires junior bankers to confirm every 3 months they haven't lined up outside jobs. Critics call this surveillance; Goldman frames it as conflict-of-interest protection.
- Q: Is Goldman's culture improving for juniors? A: Modestly. Salary increased, Saturdays nominally protected. But fundamental hours culture has not changed per recent reports.
- Q: Which Goldman division has the best work-life balance? A: Asset Management and Technology are generally considered better. Investment Banking remains grueling.

Known Weaknesses:
- Work-life balance is structurally poor in core revenue-generating divisions
- Loyalty surveillance (quarterly check-in) signals cultural dysfunction
- Junior analysts are essentially expendable in the traditional model
- Compensation per hour is not competitive with tech companies when hours are factored in
- Non-IB divisions can feel underpaid and undervalued
- Office culture described as impersonal, silent, and cold

Known Strengths:

- Compensation is exceptional for top performers in IB/Trading
- Deal access and experience is world-class
