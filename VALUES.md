# Values

These are the operating principles of Unfortunately. They are not aspirational. They are behavioral. Every decision should be traceable back to at least one of them.

---

## 1. Build Honestly

We do not exaggerate what our product does. We do not use growth hacks that mislead users about what they are getting. We do not write marketing copy that implies capabilities we don't have.

If something doesn't work well, we say so. If a feature is experimental, we label it.

---

## 2. Never Fabricate Information

Employer simulations are based on real patterns: real job postings, real rejection emails, real interview styles documented publicly. We do not invent negative things about specific companies without basis.

If we cannot verify something, we do not present it as fact.

---

## 3. Disclose AI-Generated Content

When content is generated or significantly shaped by AI, we say so when it matters. We do not pretend human judgment produced something that an algorithm produced.

This applies to employer simulations, email templates, interview questions, and any content that users might mistake for human research.

---

## 4. Users Come Before Growth Hacks

We do not send emails users didn't ask for to boost open rates. We do not use dark patterns to increase retention. We do not optimize for engagement metrics that don't correspond to user value.

If a growth tactic would embarrass us if published, we don't use it.

---

## 5. Humor Should Reveal Truth

Satire earns its place by making something real more visible, not by being funny for its own sake. Every joke should point at something true about hiring, corporate behavior, or power dynamics.

Humor that doesn't reveal anything is just noise.

---

## 6. Satire Should Punch Upward

We mock systems, not candidates. We mock corporate language, not the people who use it to survive. We mock hiring processes, not hiring managers as individuals.

The target is always power, never the powerless.

---

## 7. Employer Simulations Are Fictional

When we simulate an interview at a specific company, we are creating a fictional experience inspired by public information and general patterns. We are not claiming to accurately represent that company's internal processes.

This must be clear to users at all times.

---

## 8. Transparency Over Engagement

We would rather have fewer, better-informed users than more users who misunderstand what we are.

When transparency costs engagement, we choose transparency.

---

## 9. Build Things Worth Maintaining

We do not build features we are not prepared to maintain, support, and improve. We do not launch things we plan to abandon. We do not make promises we will not keep.

Shipping something mediocre is worse than not shipping it.

---

## 10. Trust Is the Product

Unfortunately's long-term value depends entirely on candidates trusting that we are not manipulating them. Every decision that erodes that trust is a product defect.

This applies to how we handle data, how we write copy, how we respond to criticism, and how we behave when no one is watching.
